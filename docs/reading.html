Reading the screens

Each pedal draws the same note on two screens — one over time, one by frequency — with the dry and wet signals overlaid. Here's how to read all four pictures. The traces below are drawn to explain each screen, not pulled from a live pedal.

waveform in dry vs out wet

waveform

time · x = ms → the signal arriving, moment to moment

The signal's own up-and-down as it moves through — tall is loud, and the shape carries the tone. Clipping flattens the peaks into grit; an echo repeats the whole wiggle, quieter each time.

Read the shape, not the height: same size, different edges = a different tone.

envelope in dry vs out wet

envelope

time · x = ms → the signal arriving, moment to moment

The outline of the loudness over time — the wave's swell traced out, with the fast wiggles dropped. This is where tremolo's pulse shows, or a delay's repeats stepping down one after another.

The line is a loudness contour, not the sound itself — watch it rise, dip, and repeat.

spectrum in dry vs out wet

spectrum

frequency · x = kHz → the sound split into pitches, low → high

Which pitches are actually in the sound — one spike per frequency present. A clean note is a single spike; distortion sprouts a row of new spikes (harmonics) that weren't there before.

Count the spikes — new ones are frequencies the pedal invented.

seen onoverdrivetremolo

comb in dry vs out wet

comb

frequency · x = Hz → the sound split into pitches, low → high

Not the sound — the pedal's own fingerprint: how much it lifts or cuts each frequency. A short, swept delay cuts a row of regular notches — the teeth of a comb — while the dry line sits flat at 0 dB.

Above the flat line it boosts, below it cuts. Chorus's teeth are fine and close; a flanger's are few, wide, and deep.
